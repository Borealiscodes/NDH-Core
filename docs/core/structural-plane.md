# Structural Plane  
### Constitutional Specification for UMA’s Objects, Boundaries, and Relationships

## 1. Purpose
The Structural Plane defines UMA’s core objects, entities, and relationships.  
It establishes the ontology of the system: what exists, how it is represented, and how it can be referenced across planes.

The Structural Plane ensures:
- structural clarity  
- referential stability  
- non‑collapse between layers  
- consistent object identity  
- cross‑plane coherence  

It is the foundation upon which all other UMA planes operate.

See: **[UMA Constitution](ca://s?q=Generate_UMA_Constitution)**

---

## 2. Nature of the Structural Plane
The Structural Plane is descriptive, not interpretive.  
It defines what exists, not what it means.

It is:
- non‑coercive  
- non‑speculative  
- non‑diagnostic  
- non‑evaluative  
- non‑prescriptive  

It is the constitutional map of UMA’s architecture.

See: **[Boundary Layer](ca://s?q=Generate_Boundary_Layer)**

---

## 3. Core Structural Objects

### **3.1 Entities**
Stable conceptual units.  
Entities have:
- identity  
- boundaries  
- referential anchors  
- cross‑plane visibility  

Entities cannot be reinterpreted across domains.

---

### **3.2 Structures**
Composed objects with internal logic.  
Structures define:
- arrangement  
- containment  
- adjacency  
- hierarchy (non‑coercive)  

Structures cannot collapse into governance or narrative.

---

### **3.3 Relations**
Defined links between objects.  
Relations must be:
- explicit  
- non‑coercive  
- reversible  
- structurally bounded  

Relations cannot imply authority or interpretation.

---

### **3.4 Constraints**
Rules governing object behavior.  
Constraints must:
- preserve stability  
- prevent collapse  
- maintain boundaries  

Constraints cannot override sovereignty.

See: **[Author Sovereignty Charter](ca://s?q=Explain_Author_Sovereignty)**

---

### **3.5 Identifiers**
Stable references across documents and planes.  
Identifiers must:
- remain persistent  
- remain unambiguous  
- remain non‑interpretive  
- remain structurally consistent  

Identifiers cannot imply meaning.

---

## 4. Structural Boundaries

### **4.1 Object Boundaries**
Objects must remain within their defined scope.  
No object may expand without explicit structural definition.

### **4.2 Plane Boundaries**
Objects may be visible across planes, but may not mutate across planes.

### **4.3 Narrative Boundaries**
Structural objects cannot contain narrative content.  
Narrative belongs to the Witness Literature Plane.

See: **[Witness Literature Plane](ca://s?q=Explain_witness_literature_plane)**

### **4.4 Governance Boundaries**
Structural objects cannot contain governance rules.  
Governance belongs to the Consent and Boundary layers.

---

## 5. Structural Integrity Rules

### **5.1 Non‑Interpretation**
Structural objects must be defined explicitly.  
The system may not infer missing structure.

### **5.2 Non‑Collapse**
Structural definitions cannot collapse into:
- governance  
- dynamic behavior  
- developmental sequence  
- narrative meaning  

Each domain remains distinct.

### **5.3 Referential Integrity**
All references must:
- point to valid objects  
- maintain version lineage  
- avoid circular dependency unless explicitly defined  

### **5.4 Structural Minimalism**
The Structural Plane defines only what must exist.  
Speculation is prohibited.

---

## 6. Cross‑Plane Relationships

The Structural Plane interacts with:

### **6.1 Documentation Plane**
Defines how structures are recorded.  
Documentation cannot alter structure.

See: **[Documentation Plane](ca://s?q=Explain_documentation_plane)**

### **6.2 Governance Plane**
Defines constraints on structures.  
Governance cannot mutate structure.

### **6.3 Developmental Plane**
Defines when structures appear.  
Development cannot reinterpret structure.

### **6.4 Dynamic Plane**
Defines how structures behave over time.  
Dynamics cannot collapse structure.

### **6.5 Integrative Plane**
Ensures coherence across structures.  
Integration cannot override boundaries.

---

## 7. Structural Operations

Valid operations:
- mapping  
- diagramming  
- listing components  
- defining boundaries  
- describing flows  

Invalid operations:
- assigning authority  
- defining rules  
- implying hierarchy  
- embedding ethical meaning  

These prohibitions are constitutional.

---

## 8. Structural Stability

The Structural Plane must remain:
- predictable  
- layered  
- bounded  
- reversible  
- non‑coercive  

Structural instability routes to:
- Coherence Plane  
- Boundary Layer  
- Witness Plane  

See: **[Coherence Plane](ca://s?q=Explain_coherence_plane)**

---

## 9. Summary

The Structural Plane is UMA’s constitutional ontology.  
It defines:
- what exists  
- how it is arranged  
- how it is referenced  
- how it remains stable  

It ensures:
- structural clarity  
- referential stability  
- non‑collapse  
- cross‑plane coherence  
- sovereignty protection  

The Structural Plane cannot be overridden.
